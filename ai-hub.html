CallHippo AI Hub

How I designed an AI knowledge layer that gives support agents instant, accurate answers — without switching tabs or searching docs

Problem

Support agents were answering the same questions differently — because finding the right answer took too long

CallHippo serves support teams managing high volumes of inbound customer conversations across WhatsApp, chat, and other channels. The questions customers asked were largely predictable — pricing, integrations, onboarding steps, product features. The answers existed somewhere. But during a live conversation, agents couldn't access them fast enough to be useful.

The typical workflow: a customer asks a question, the agent pauses the conversation, opens a browser tab, searches an internal doc or website, reads through it, composes a response, and types it back — all while the customer waits. For complex products with large knowledge bases, this added significant lag to every interaction.

The result: slower response times, inconsistent answers across agents, and customers who noticed the gaps. The AI capability to surface relevant knowledge instantly was available. What didn't exist was a designed system for importing, managing, and surfacing that knowledge where agents actually needed it — inside the conversation itself.

Why This Problem? — Auditing the Current Process

Before moving to solutions, I spent two weeks understanding how support teams were currently handling knowledge retrieval during live conversations. I interviewed 11 people — support agents, team leads, and operations managers.

Two distinct roles — each with a different relationship to in-conversation knowledge

SA

Support Agent

"I know the answer exists somewhere — in the docs, on the website, in a PDF someone shared in Slack three months ago. But I can't find it fast enough. So I either make my best guess or ask my manager, and the customer is just waiting."

SA

Support Team Lead

"I've seen two agents give completely different answers to the same pricing question in the same hour. It's not a training problem. It's a retrieval problem. The knowledge is there — they just can't get to it in time."

The existing experience was failing both roles

Knowledge lived everywhere except where agents worked

Docs were in Google Drive, pricing was on the website, integration guides were in PDFs. None of it was accessible from inside the chat interface.

Search was too slow for live conversations

Even when agents knew where to look, switching context, finding the right document, and scanning for the answer took long enough that customers experienced it as hesitation.

Inconsistency was invisible to management

Different agents giving different answers to the same question produced no alert, no flag, no signal. It was only caught when a customer complained.

No feedback loop between customer questions and knowledge gaps

When an agent couldn't find an answer, that gap was never recorded anywhere. The same question would stump the next agent too.

Research — Understanding Each Role's Real Pain

I ran remote observation sessions where agents handled live conversations while I watched their tab-switching behavior. The patterns were consistent and revealing.

Insight 01

Agents weren't slow — the system was. Speed was the only thing that mattered during a live conversation.

The bottleneck was purely retrieval. The moment an agent had to leave the chat interface to find an answer, the conversation stalled.

Insight 02

Agents trusted a source more when it was surfaced in context — not when they had to search for it.

When agents received a suggested answer alongside the customer's message, they responded faster and with more confidence — even when the underlying content was the same.

Insight 03

Admins had no scalable way to keep knowledge current.

When product features changed or pricing updated, there was no mechanism to push that change to agents. Knowledge became stale with no visible indicator.

Insight 04

The import step was the biggest barrier to getting started.

Admins described the import experience as technically opaque. There was no clear path from "I have a website" or "I have a PDF" to "my AI knows about my product."

Core Challenges Summarized

Support Agents

Answers existed but were unreachable during live conversations — making every complex question a performance of confidence they didn't have.

Team Leads & Admins

No reliable, maintainable system for keeping knowledge accurate — and no visibility into where the gaps were.

Brainstorming & Scoping — Choosing the Right MVP

After synthesizing research, I ran a prioritization session with the PM and engineering lead. We mapped potential features against agent impact and setup complexity.

We asked: How Might We...

  • Surface the right answer at the right moment — without the agent having to ask for it?
  • Make importing company knowledge as simple as pasting a URL or uploading a file?
  • Give agents a way to query the knowledge base directly from inside a conversation?
  • Help admins see which questions the AI couldn't answer — so they know what to add?

Four design priorities emerged as the MVP core

Frictionless Import

URL and PDF import that requires no technical configuration.

AI Training Status Visibility

Clear feedback on whether imported content is ready to use.

Reply with AI

Letting agents generate an AI-drafted response directly from the message card.

Edit Before You Send

Giving agents full control to review, adjust, or send the AI-generated response in one step.

Design — Feature by Feature

Feature 01

Import — URL and Document Ingestion

The Problem it solves

The biggest barrier to using AI Hub wasn't the AI — it was getting content into it. Admins had knowledge on their website, in PDFs, and in help articles. There was no simple path from any of those formats to a trained AI model. The import experience was the gate everything else depended on.

Design Decisions

I designed the import flow around two entry points — website URL and document upload — accessible from a single prominent Import button in the top-right. Once imported, each document appears as a named row in the AI Hub table — visible, labeled, and timestamped. The Status column shows "Ready" in green when the AI has finished training on that content, giving admins a clear, unambiguous signal that the knowledge is live. The table format was chosen deliberately over a card grid: admins managing large knowledge bases needed to scan and manage at scale, not interact with individual items one at a time.

Impact

The goal was to make "my AI knows about my product" a step that takes minutes, not a technical project. An admin who can paste a URL and see "Ready" within the same session is an admin who will actually maintain their knowledge base — rather than importing once and abandoning it.

Feature 02

Reply with AI

The Problem it solves

Agents were leaving the chat to find answers — switching tabs, searching docs, reading through pages — while customers waited. Even when they found the right information, the act of retrieving it broke the flow of the conversation. What agents needed wasn't a separate tool. They needed assistance that lived exactly where the work was already happening.

Design Decisions

The flow starts on the message itself. When an agent clicks the arrow icon on a message card, a context menu appears with familiar actions — Reply, Forward, Copy — and one new one: Reply with AI, badged distinctly to signal it produces a different kind of output. One click from there triggers the AI to read the message, query the knowledge base, and generate a suggested response.

That response surfaces as an inline card directly below the customer's message — anchored in the thread, not in a sidebar. The card shows the full AI-generated answer with two actions: Edit and Send. Agents who trust the answer send immediately. Agents who want to adjust — change the tone, add context, correct a detail — edit before it goes out. Neither action requires leaving the conversation or switching any context.

The two-action model was a deliberate trust decision. Agents should always feel in control of what the customer receives — especially when the content was generated by AI, not written by them. The "AI Assistant is here to help." header makes the origin of the content explicit every time, so agents never accidentally send something they haven't consciously approved.

Impact

The goal was to make the entire journey — from reading a customer message to sending an accurate, knowledge-grounded reply — happen without the agent ever leaving the thread. A trigger that fits the existing workflow, a response that appears in context, and a send path that requires no extra navigation. The feature works because it asks nothing new of the agent. It just makes what they were already doing faster and more accurate.

Feature 03

Knowledge Base Status Table

Document Name Status Created At
Product Integration Guide ↗ Ready 9-2-2026 12:12
Pricing & Plans FAQ ↗ Ready 9-2-2026 12:11
Onboarding Documentation ↗ Processing 9-2-2026 12:10

The Problem it solves

Admins importing content had no visibility into whether the AI had actually processed it. Was the training complete? Was this version of the doc the current one? When pricing changed and a new PDF was uploaded, was the old one still influencing answers? Without clear status signals, admins couldn't trust what the AI knew — and couldn't manage it confidently.

Design Decisions

The AI Hub table surfaces three columns that matter: Document Name, Status, and Created At. Status is rendered as a color-coded label — green "Ready" for trained content — making the state of each document readable in a single glance. The Created At timestamp gives admins a temporal reference: they can see immediately if an article was imported before a product update and flag it for refresh. The delete action on each row makes content management explicit and low-effort. The table is paginated with a 10-per-page default and a page-size control, designed to scale gracefully as knowledge bases grow.

Impact

The goal was to give admins full visibility and control over what the AI knew — and confidence that what was marked "Ready" was actually ready. A well-maintained knowledge base is the foundation of every accurate answer the AI surfaces. The table design made maintenance feel manageable rather than daunting.

Results

AI Hub was designed for support teams handling high-volume inbound conversations where the speed and accuracy of every answer directly affected customer experience.

The core shift the design aimed to create was moving knowledge retrieval from a parallel, context-switching task — something agents did outside the conversation — to an integrated, invisible layer that delivered answers inside the conversation, at the moment they were needed.

For admins, the goal was equally significant: replacing a technically opaque import process with a simple two-path flow — URL or document — that any non-technical operations lead could manage and maintain. A knowledge base that's easy to build is a knowledge base that stays current.

One outcome that emerged beyond the original scope: the AI Hub panel began functioning as a training accelerator for new agents. Junior support members who had only been onboarded for days were responding with the same accuracy as experienced agents — because the knowledge they needed was surfaced in real time. A use case never planned, but one that reflects how ambient, in-context intelligence can compress ramp time in ways that structured training programs cannot.

What I Learned

  1. 1. The best AI feature is the one that fits where work already happens.

    Putting "Reply with AI" inside the message context menu — where agents were already clicking — meant zero behavior change. The feature got used because it asked nothing new. The trigger was already there. We just added intelligence to it.

  2. 2. The import experience determines whether the feature gets used at all.

    No matter how good the AI is, if admins can't get their knowledge into the system simply and confidently, the feature doesn't exist in practice. Designing import as a two-step flow — paste URL or upload file, see status turn green — was the prerequisite that made everything else possible.

  3. 3. Agents need to feel in control of what the customer receives

    The Edit and Send split wasn't about adding a step — it was about building trust. Agents who knew they could review before sending were more willing to use AI assistance at all. Control is what made the feature feel safe enough to rely on.
