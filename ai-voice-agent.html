CallHippo AI Voice Agent

How I designed an AI voice system that handles every inbound call — automatically, 24/7

Design Goals

Success metrics were defined during scoping in collaboration with the PM. Baselines were gathered from walkthroughs and interviews with 12 support managers, team leads, and SMB owners.

Problem

Support teams were losing customers in the moments between a call coming in and a human picking up

CallHippo serves thousands of customer support operations fielding inbound calls every day — billing questions, product FAQs, appointment confirmations, order status checks. These were predictable, repeatable inquiries. The answers existed. But there was no system to deliver them automatically.

When no agent was available — after hours, during peak volume, or when the team was understaffed — callers hit voicemail, got placed in long queues, or dropped off entirely. Every missed call was a missed resolution. Every long wait was a damaged relationship.

The challenge wasn't just building the AI — it was designing a setup experience so simple that any non-technical support lead could deploy it confidently, without developer help, in a single session.

Why This Problem? — Understanding the Gap

Before moving to solutions, I spent two weeks understanding how support teams were currently managing inbound call volume. I interviewed 12 people — support managers, team leads, and SMB owners running their own customer service operations.

Two distinct roles — each with a different relationship to inbound call management

SM

Support Team Lead

"We miss a huge chunk of calls after 6pm. Those customers don't call back. They just leave. I know an AI could handle most of those questions — I just don't know how to set it up."

SO

SMB Owner

"I'm the only support person. When I'm on a call, the next customer gets nothing. I need something that can at least answer and hold them until I'm free."

What both roles needed — and didn't have

A way to handle routine calls without a human picking up

Billing questions, product FAQs, appointment confirmations, and order status checks were predictable and repeatable — but every single one required a human to answer. There was no automated layer between the caller and the queue.

A setup experience built for non-technical users

Support leads and SMB owners aren't developers. They needed to define what the agent knows, how it sounds, and which number it answers — without writing scripts, configuring logic trees, or involving engineering.

Confidence in what the agent would say before going live

The biggest fear wasn't the AI getting something wrong — it was the customer noticing. Support leads needed to hear and verify the agent's voice and responses before a single real call came in.

24/7 coverage without 24/7 staffing

After-hours calls were the highest-priority gap for every team interviewed. Customers who couldn't reach support after 6pm didn't call back — they left. An always-on AI layer was the only viable solution for teams that couldn't staff around the clock.

Research — Understanding Each Role's Real Pain

I ran remote interviews and walkthroughs where support leads described their current call management workflows while thinking aloud. The same patterns surfaced consistently across team sizes and industries.

Insight 01

The biggest fear wasn't the AI getting something wrong — it was the customer noticing.

Support leads could tolerate an imperfect answer. What they couldn't tolerate was an agent that sounded unnatural or robotic — because that reflected directly on their brand.

Insight 02

Support managers think in call scenarios, not configuration logic.

When asked what they wanted the agent to handle, users described situations — "a customer asking about pricing" or "someone calling after hours" — not settings. The design had to meet that mental model.

Insight 03

Knowledge was the foundation — but nobody framed it that way.

Every support lead had FAQs, help docs, and product guides. What they didn't have was a way to connect that knowledge to an AI agent. They thought of it as a technical problem — not a content problem they already had the answer to.

Insight 04

After-hours coverage was the primary motivation for every person interviewed.

Without exception, every support lead and SMB owner cited missed after-hours calls as their core problem. The design had to make this the default use case — not an advanced setting.

Core Challenges Summarized

Support Team Leads

Needed 24/7 call coverage but had no path to deploy it without developer involvement.

The gap wasn't motivation — it was a setup experience that felt like infrastructure, not a business tool.

SMB Owners

Every call they couldn't answer was a customer lost — with no fallback between "answer it yourself" and "hire more staff."

They needed something they could set up alone, in minutes, with zero technical background.

Brainstorming & Scoping — Choosing the Right MVP

After synthesizing research, I ran a prioritization session with the PM and engineering lead. We mapped potential features against support team needs and build complexity.

We asked: How Might We...

  • Let any support lead create and deploy an AI agent — without writing a single line of code?
  • Ground every AI response in knowledge the team already owns — FAQs, help docs, product guides?
  • Give support leads confidence in how the agent sounds before it speaks to a single real customer?
  • Make the entire setup completable in one session — name, knowledge, voice, and number in one flow?

Four design priorities emerged as the MVP core

Single-Popup Setup Flow

Every configuration decision in one place, in a fixed sequence — name, knowledge, voice, number. No page-switching, no drop-off.

Knowledge Base as the Answer Engine

Ground every agent response in content the support team already owns. Upload a doc or select an existing one — no technical configuration required.

Voice & Behaviour Configuration

Let support leads hear the agent's voice before committing, and set its conversational style — Friendly, Empathetic, or Professional — in plain language.

Number Allocation

A visual card grid that makes number selection spatial, scannable, and unambiguous — replacing the confusion of dropdowns and region codes.

User Flow

Before designing any screen, I mapped the full setup journey — from a support lead clicking "Create Agent" to an AI agent going live. This flow revealed that every configuration decision needed to live in a single popup, in a deliberate sequence, so non-technical users could deploy without ever feeling lost.

Setup — Create Agent
Set Agent Name → Upload / Select Knowledge Base → Select Voice & Behaviour → Allocate Number → Agent Created ✓

Key Design Decision from This Flow

Mapping this flow made one constraint clear: every decision had to live inside a single popup in a fixed sequence. Splitting setup across multiple pages would create drop-off. Placing Knowledge Base before Voice & Behaviour was intentional — an agent needs to know what it knows before deciding how it sounds. Sequence was the design.

Design — Feature by Feature

Feature 01

Agent Setup — 3-Step Creation Flow

The Problem it solves

Support leads needed to go from zero to a live AI agent — in a single session, without developer help. There was no guided path to define what the agent knew, how it sounded, or which number it answered. The design challenge was making every decision feel simple, sequential, and reversible.

Design Decisions

I consolidated the entire setup into a single popup with a fixed 3-step sequence — each step with one job.

  • Step 1 — Identity & Knowledge: The agent's name and knowledge base are set together first. Naming the agent makes it feel like a team member. Selecting the knowledge base immediately after grounds every subsequent decision in what the agent actually knows. Support leads can pick an existing doc or upload a new one directly — no context switching.
  • Step 2 — Voice & Behaviour: Voice is a named table with accent and a play button so leads hear the voice before committing. Behaviour — Friendly, Empathetic, or Professional — is a separate selector because how the agent sounds and how it comes across are different decisions that support leads naturally think about separately.
  • Step 3 — Number Allocation: Placed last intentionally. A support lead who has already named their agent, grounded it in knowledge, and set its voice is far more confident making this final decision. The number grid displays each option as a labeled tile — spatial and scannable rather than a dropdown.

Impact

Consolidating setup into a single 3-step popup reduced average agent creation time from 15–20 minutes to approximately 6 minutes — and eliminated the mid-flow abandonment that had prevented most non-technical support leads from deploying at all. The sequence made the difference: each step built confidence for the next.

Feature 02

Welcome Message

The Problem it solves

The first thing a caller hears determines whether they stay on the line or hang up. Support leads had no way to define or preview this greeting — so it defaulted to a generic, unbranded response that made the AI feel like a system, not an extension of their team.

Design Decisions

I designed the welcome message as a foregrounded, toggle-enabled block with an editable text field and an inline preview mechanism. The example message serves as both a default and a writing guide. The toggle makes activation explicit — the agent only greets callers the way the team intended, and the team can see exactly what that greeting is before a single call comes in.

Impact

A support lead who has written and heard their own greeting is meaningfully more confident deploying the agent than one who hasn't. The welcome message became the moment ownership transferred from "system I'm setting up" to "agent that represents my brand."

Feature 03

Knowledge Base

The Problem it solves

An AI voice agent is only as good as what it knows. Support teams had existing FAQs, product guides, and help documentation — but no way to connect any of it to the agent's responses. Without a grounded knowledge source, the agent would give generic, unreliable answers that eroded customer trust.

Design Decisions

Knowledge Base is the second step in the setup flow — placed immediately after identity, before voice or number. This sequencing was intentional: grounding the agent in real support content before configuring anything else ensures every subsequent decision is made with a clear picture of what the agent actually knows. The inline "create a new Knowledge Base" link keeps users in the flow if they need to create one from scratch.

Impact

Making knowledge base connection a required, foregrounded step — not optional — was the single decision that most directly determined call quality post-launch. Agents configured with real knowledge handled inquiries accurately. The knowledge base wasn't a feature. It was the product.

Feature 04

Voice and Behavior

The Problem it solves

Support teams needed the agent to feel like a natural extension of their brand — not a generic system voice. And they needed its conversational style to match how their human agents already spoke to customers.

Design Decisions

Voice Preference is a named, structured table — each entry shows a voice name, gender, and accent with a play button for preview. Behavior is a separate, clearly labeled block with a plain-language selector — Friendly, Professional, Empathetic. Separating voice from behavior was an intentional IA decision: they are distinct dimensions of how the agent presents. Support leads think about "how it sounds" and "how it comes across" as separate questions — the design mirrors that mental model.

Impact

Support leads who heard their agent's voice before going live reported significantly higher confidence in deployment. The preview step — hearing it before committing — was the moment the agent stopped feeling like a risk and started feeling like a team member.

Results

Routine Call Containment

0% → ~54%

Billing, FAQs, appointment and order calls handled by AI — agents now focus only on complex cases

AI Voice Agent launched to support teams who needed 24/7 call coverage without 24/7 staffing. The core shift was moving from reactive availability — answering calls only when a human happened to be free — to continuous, automatic coverage grounded in real support content and deliberate brand voice.

For support leads, the equally significant outcome was confidence: every team that deployed had already named their agent, connected their knowledge, and heard their greeting before a single real call came in. Trust was built during setup — not after a bad call.

One outcome beyond the original scope: call disposition data began informing knowledge base improvements. Teams used resolution patterns to identify which questions the agent couldn't answer well — and updated their content accordingly. A feedback loop that wasn't designed for, but one that made the entire agent smarter over time.

What I Learned

  1. 1. Sequence is a design decision.

    The order of steps in the setup flow wasn't arbitrary — it was the design. Knowledge before voice before number meant each step built confidence for the next. A support lead who had heard their agent's voice before allocating a number felt ownership, not anxiety.

  2. 2. The knowledge base is the product, not a prerequisite.

    Teams that connected strong support documentation to their agent saw fundamentally different call quality than those who didn't. Foregrounding this in the setup flow wasn't just good IA — it was a product decision. What the agent knows determines everything it can do.

  3. 3. For 0→1 products, trust is the real design problem.

    Building a new capability from scratch means users have no prior experience to anchor their confidence. Every design decision — the preview button, the fixed sequence, the named voice table — was oriented around one goal: giving support leads enough trust in the system to press "Create Agent" the first time.
