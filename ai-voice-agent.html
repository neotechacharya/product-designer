How I designed an AI voice system that handles every inbound call — automatically, 24/7

Problem

Support teams were losing customers in the moments between a call coming in and a human picking up

CallHippo serves thousands of customer support operations fielding inbound calls every day — billing questions, product FAQs, appointment confirmations, order status checks. These were predictable, repeatable inquiries. The answers existed. But there was no system to deliver them automatically.

When no agent was available — after hours, during peak volume, or when the team was understaffed — callers hit voicemail, got placed in long queues, or dropped off entirely. Every missed call was a missed resolution. Every long wait was a damaged relationship.

The AI capability to handle these conversations existed within CallHippo. The problem was that support leads had no practical way to deploy it. Configuring an inbound voice agent required navigating a fragmented setup experience built for technical users — not the support managers and operations leads who actually needed it.

Why This Problem? — Auditing the Current Process

Before moving to solutions, I spent two weeks understanding how support teams were currently managing inbound call volume. I interviewed 12 people — support managers, team leads, and SMB owners running their own customer service operations.

Two distinct roles — each with a different relationship to inbound call management

SM

Support Team Lead

"We miss a huge chunk of calls after 6pm. Those customers don't call back. They just leave. I know an AI could handle most of those questions — I just don't know how to set it up without breaking our existing routing."

SO

SMB Owner

"I'm the only support person. When I'm on a call, the next customer gets nothing. I need something that can at least answer and hold them until I'm free."

The existing experience was failing both roles

No confidence in what the agent would say

Support leads needed to know exactly how the agent would respond before going live. There was no way to preview or verify this without a live test call.

Routing felt risky

Misrouting a customer to the wrong team was worse than no routing at all. The existing configuration gave no indication of how rules would behave in real scenarios.

Welcome message was an afterthought

The first thing a caller hears shapes the entire interaction. In the existing flow, the welcome message was a single unlabelled text field — no guidance, no preview, no defaults.

After-hours coverage had no dedicated design

Setting the agent to handle calls outside business hours required understanding scheduling logic that wasn't documented or explained anywhere in the product.

Research — Understanding Each Role's Real Pain

I ran remote walkthroughs where support leads attempted to configure an inbound agent using the existing product while thinking aloud. The same friction points surfaced consistently.

Insight 01

The biggest fear wasn't the AI getting something wrong — it was the customer noticing.

Support leads could tolerate an imperfect answer. What they couldn't tolerate was an agent that sounded unnatural or robotic — because that reflected directly on their brand.

Insight 02

Support managers think in call scenarios, not configuration logic.

When asked what they wanted the agent to handle, users described situations — not settings. The mental model gap was the core design problem.

Insight 03

Routing was the highest-stakes decision — and the least supported one.

Getting a customer to the right person mattered most, yet the routing configuration was the most technically complex part of setup. Users either skipped it or got it wrong.

Insight 04

24/7 coverage was the primary motivation — but it wasn't being designed for.

Every support lead mentioned after-hours coverage as the core reason they wanted an AI agent. Yet nothing in the existing setup was oriented around this use case.

Core Challenges Summarized

Support Team Leads

No way to know what the agent would say before it went live — and no confidence it would route correctly when it did.

Setup felt like developer infrastructure, not a business tool.

SMB Owners

No path from "I want to cover inbound calls" to "my agent is live" without significant technical friction.

The gap between intent and deployment was too wide for solo operators.

Brainstorming & Scoping — Choosing the Right MVP

After synthesizing research, I ran a prioritization session with the PM and engineering lead. We mapped potential improvements against support team needs and build complexity.

We asked: How Might We...

  • Let support leads define what the agent says — in plain language, without scripting?
  • Give teams confidence that routing will work correctly before going live?
  • Make after-hours coverage the default assumption, not a hidden advanced setting?
  • Ensure every caller hears something that sounds like the brand — not a generic bot?

Four design priorities emerged as the MVP core

Welcome Message Design

Make the first thing a caller hears a deliberate, brand-aligned, verifiable decision.

Knowledge Base as the Answer Engine

Ground every agent response in content the support team already owns and controls.

Call Routing with Scenario Clarity

Let teams define routing rules in terms of call scenarios, not logic trees.

Voice and Behavior Configuration

Ensure the agent's tone and personality are set before anything else.

Design — Feature by Feature

Feature 01

Allocate Number

The Problem it solves

Assigning a phone number to an inbound agent was one of the top sources of setup confusion. Users didn't understand region availability, couldn't tell which number was active vs. unassigned, and weren't sure if selecting a number meant calls would immediately start routing through it. The result was hesitation, wrong selections, and a high volume of "how do I assign my number" support tickets.

Design Decisions

I redesigned number allocation as a visual card grid — each number displayed as a labeled tile showing region name and phone number, organized spatially rather than as a dropdown list. The selected number is highlighted with a clear border accent, making the active state unambiguous at a glance. A search bar sits in the top-right for teams managing multiple numbers across regions. The grid layout was a deliberate choice over a list: it makes selection feel like picking from a catalog — spatial, scannable, and reversible — rather than committing to a configuration. The descriptive copy at the top ("all incoming calls to the selected number will be answered by this AI Voice Agent") does one critical job — it tells users exactly what activating this number means before they make the choice.

Impact

The goal was to eliminate the anxiety of number assignment entirely. A design that is spatial, clearly labeled by region, and visually confirms the active selection was expected to significantly reduce misassignments and the support contacts that followed them.

Feature 02

Welcome Message

The Problem it solves

The first thing a caller hears determines whether they stay on the line or hang up. In the existing product, the welcome message was a blank text field with no guidance, no preview, and no defaults. Most support leads left it as a generic placeholder they hadn't written.

Design Decisions

I designed the welcome message as a foregrounded, toggle-enabled block with an editable text field and an inline preview mechanism. The example message serves as both a default and a writing guide. The toggle makes activation explicit — the agent only greets callers the way the team intended, and the team can see exactly what that greeting is before a single call comes in. Surfacing this early in the settings page was a deliberate IA decision: it's the lowest-friction, highest-confidence first step a support lead can take.

Impact

The goal was to make the welcome message feel like a brand decision, not a technical field. A support lead who has written and heard their own greeting is meaningfully more confident deploying the agent than one who hasn't.

Feature 03

Knowledge Base

The Problem it solves

An inbound voice agent is only as good as what it knows. Support teams had existing documentation and FAQs — but no way to connect it to the agent's responses. Without a grounded knowledge source, agents either gave generic answers or escalated unnecessarily to human agents.

Design Decisions

I positioned Knowledge Base as the second configuration decision — immediately after agent identity, before voice or routing. The selection is a single dropdown tied to content the team has already created, with a direct link to create a new one if needed. This sequencing was intentional: grounding the agent in real support content before configuring anything else ensures every subsequent decision is made with a clear picture of what the agent actually knows. The inline "create a new Knowledge Base" link prevents users from leaving the flow to complete a prerequisite step.

Impact

The goal was to make the agent's knowledge the foundation of the setup experience — not a step that gets skipped because it felt optional. A support agent configured with a real knowledge base handles inquiries accurately; one without it creates more escalations than it prevents.

Feature 04

Voice and Behavior

The Problem it solves

Support teams needed the agent to feel like a natural extension of their brand — not a generic system voice. And they needed its conversational style to match how their human agents already spoke to customers.

Design Decisions

Voice Preference is a named, structured table — each entry shows a voice name, gender, and accent — making configuration feel like defining a team member's profile rather than selecting a system setting. Behavior is a separate, clearly labeled block with a plain-language selector — Friendly, Professional, Empathetic. Separating voice from behavior was an intentional IA decision: they are distinct dimensions of how the agent presents. Support leads think about "how it sounds" and "how it comes across" as separate questions — the design mirrors that mental model.

Impact

The goal was to give support teams ownership of how their agent showed up in every call — so that when a customer interacted with the AI, it felt like a natural part of the support experience, not a system bolted on from outside.

Results

AI Voice Agent — Inbound was designed for support teams who needed 24/7 call coverage without 24/7 staffing.

The core shift the design aimed to create was moving support operations from reactive availability — answering calls only when a human happened to be free — to continuous, consistent customer coverage grounded in real support content and deliberate brand voice.

For support leads, the goal was equally significant: replacing the anxiety of an unmonitored phone line with a system they had genuinely configured, heard, and trusted before it ever spoke to a customer. Every design decision — the welcome message preview, the knowledge base as a foundation, the explicit routing toggle — was oriented around building that trust during setup, so it didn't have to be earned after a bad call.

One outcome that emerged beyond the original scope: Call Disposition data began informing knowledge base improvements. Support teams used resolution patterns to identify the questions their agent couldn't answer well — and updated their content accordingly. A feedback loop that wasn't designed for, but one that reflects how a well-structured classification system can make the entire agent smarter over time.

What I Learned

  1. 1. The first thing a user configures sets the emotional tone for everything that follows.

    Designing the Welcome Message as the opening act of setup — rather than an afterthought — changed how users related to the entire process. A support lead who has written and heard their own greeting feels ownership before they've touched a single advanced setting. Sequence is a design decision.

  2. 2. The knowledge base is the product, not a prerequisite.

    Teams that connected strong support documentation to their agent saw fundamentally different outcomes than those who didn't. Foregrounding this early in the setup flow wasn't just good IA — it was a product decision. What the agent knows determines everything it can do.

  3. 3. Routing complexity should not block deployment.

    Separating the decision to enable routing from the configuration of routing rules was the single highest-impact IA change. It let teams go live with basic coverage immediately, and refine routing logic with confidence once they'd seen the agent in action.

  4. 4. The knowledge base is the product, not a prerequisite.

    Teams that connected strong support documentation to their agent saw fundamentally different outcomes. Foregrounding this in the setup flow was a design choice with product-wide implications.
