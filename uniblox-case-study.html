/01 The brief

A landing page that isn't selling its product.

Uniblox is the e-commerce layer between insurance carriers and the people who enroll in their products. Two platforms — Group Underwriting and Enrollment — handle the fragmented work that today lives in spreadsheets, PDFs, and broker emails.

The existing first fold said the words but never showed the work. A generic dashboard mock, a one-line headline, and a "Book a demo" button made the same promise every B2B SaaS hero on the internet was already making.

The brief was simple. Make the first fold prove the product. Replace the abstract claim with a 10-second story that someone could understand without reading a word.

/02 Anatomy of the old hero

Five things the old fold wasn't doing.

It looked fine. That was the problem. Every choice was a default — a dark gradient, a centered headline, a static dashboard mock, a single CTA. Strip away the brand and you couldn't tell what the company sold.

/04 Design principles

Six rules I held the work to.

These weren't decorative. Every component on the page got measured against them — if a choice didn't pass two or three of these, it was reworked or cut.

→ 01

Show, never just say.

Where the old hero claimed "AI-first" with a lavender pill, the new hero proves it: a live audit table that ticks 47 dirty data errors down to zero, a scan beam that crosses the rows, a counter that drains in real time. The viewer doesn't read about AI processing data — they watch it happen.

Rule applied 11 times
→ 02

One narrative. Two platforms.

The product is fragmented in the market — most carriers see only Underwriting or only Enrollment. The hero treats them as a single 8-second story: dirty census comes in, AI cleans it, quote goes to the carrier, the employee enrolls, the policy issues. Two tabs, one continuous payoff.

→ 03

Editorial, not SaaS-default.

Most B2B heroes converge on the same look — Inter, purple gradient, glassmorphic cards, dashboard mock. The new hero commits to an editorial light theme with cream-paper grain, an aurora gradient mesh, Poppins paired with IBM Plex Mono, and headline composition that reads more like a magazine cover than a software pitch.

→ 04

Motion serves the story, not the brand.

Every animation in the demo earns its presence. The 3-second dwell on dirty data is intentional — viewers need time to register the problem before the solution kicks in. The scan beam syncs with the progress bar. The counter ends exactly when the success state lands. Nothing animates for the sake of looking animated.

→ 05

Real names. Real data shape.

The demo isn't lorem ipsum. It's Brightline_Census.xlsx with 240 lives, going to MetLife, with realistic dirty-data shapes — Excel serial dates (33799), missing fields (—), Indian comma format (1,28,000). Anyone in the industry recognizes the artifacts immediately.

→ 06

Performance is part of the aesthetic.

The demo doesn't run until the user scrolls to it (IntersectionObserver, threshold 0.35). All in-panel animations are paused via animation-play-state until the section enters the viewport. prefers-reduced-motion collapses every animation duration to 0.01ms. The hero loads instantly and respects user intent.

/05 Headline as composition

A "from X to Y" sentence wants to be three lines.

The new headline reads as a journey statement, not a slogan. Editorial design treats journeys spatially — three intentional lines, each a meaning unit, with two italic emphasis dots that map to the two platform tabs of the demo cycling below.

Automate your insurance
from Underwriting
to Enrollment

Three lines, descending widths

23 / 17 / 13 characters per line. Centered, this forms a downward-pointing triangle — a classic magazine-cover composition that says "converging to a result" without a single decorative line.

Two italic-blue emphasis words

"Underwriting" and "Enrollment" pull the eye and visually mirror the two tabs of the demo below. Cyan dots after each italic become journey waypoints, not periods.

The dot replaces the period

Earlier drafts had a literal period after "Enrollment". Two terminus marks (cyan dot + period) fought each other. The fix: drop the period. The brand dot serves as the natural end. Same pattern as the original "Insurance•" headline this replaced.

Type system

Display: Poppins 300, 66px, line-height 1.04, letter-spacing -0.04em. Italic ems pick up color: --c-blue. text-wrap: balance as a safety net.

/06 The 8-second story

Each tab is a complete narrative arc.

The demo runs for exactly 8 seconds per panel, then hands off to the next. That's not a number I picked for symmetry — it's the time it takes for the human eye to register a problem, watch it resolve, and feel the payoff.

0.0 — 0.5s

Rows reveal

5 employees stagger in from Brightline_Census.xlsx, each carrying its messy original value.

0.5 — 3.5s

Dwell on the mess

Three seconds of static — intentional. The viewer needs time to register the problem: Excel serial dates, missing fields, Indian comma format, last-first names.

3.5s

AI activates

Scan beam descends. Progress bar starts. Counter starts ticking 47 → 0. All three sync to the same start time.

3.5 — 5.5s

Cross-fade transformation

Each row's messy value blurs out and the clean value blurs in — same cell, no arrow, no diff-style strikethrough. AI capability tags pop into place.

5.5s

Quote-ready

Counter hits 0. Status text morphs from "processing 240 lives" to "✓ quote-ready · sent to MetLife." The accent color flips to brand blue.

6.0 — 7.6s

Benefits surface

Four pills fade in: −50% prep, auto-scaling, no-code rules, <1wk deploy. The actual product proof, on-screen.

8.0s

Handoff

Tab switches to Enrollment. Anika Rao's flow begins. Same story, different actor.

Phase 01 · Problem

Show the mess

Dwell long enough that the viewer understands. The temptation is to skip ahead — but if the problem isn't legible, the solution is meaningless.

Phase 02 · Process

Synchronize the work

Scan beam, progress bar, counter, row transforms — all sync to one start time and one end time. Throughput is felt as simultaneity.

Phase 03 · Payoff

Land the win

Status morph, color flip, benefit pills. The viewer should feel something change in the room. Brief enough to honor the rhythm — long enough to register.

/07 Motion details

The animations the user doesn't notice.

Most of the motion in this hero is invisible — and that's the point. Good interaction motion respects intent, signals state, and disappears once it's done its job.

Hover bob · scroll cue

The chevron tells you it's a scroll.

"See how it works" doesn't say the word "scroll" anywhere. The chevron animates 2px down on a 1.1s loop only on hover — calm at rest, alive on intent. cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1).

Sparkle twinkle · presence

The sparkle isn't holding still.

The eyebrow's ✨ pulses scale 1 → 1.18 → 0.92 with a slight ±8° rotation, 2.6s loop. Replaces the old "live" pill — same job (signal aliveness), more characterful.

Scan beam · progress

The scan beam is doing math.

2-second descent over the audit table starting at the 3.5s dwell mark. Locked to the progress-bar fill so the visual scan and the percentage move as one signal.

priya Priya Singh
Cross-fade · transformation

Same cell, different value.

Diff cells use grid-template-areas: "swap" so before/after share one slot. Messy state blurs out, clean state blurs in — no arrow, no strikethrough on the final state. Reads as becoming, not replacing.

Tab indicator · cycle progress

One bar, two signals.

The 2px line under the tabs serves two readings — the dark portion shows which tab is active, the cyan portion shows how much of the cycle has elapsed. Same baseline, different masks.

IntersectionObserver · gate

Wait until they're watching.

The whole demo is paused via animation-play-state until .window hits 35% viewport visibility. One-shot — once armed, it runs. No re-triggering on scroll-up jitter.
